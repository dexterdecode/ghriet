I think the driving force for the Microgrids will be local activists but also the operations and management contracts can have percentage of profit built into it. This will incentivize the contractors to roll out the Microgrids across the country. Eventually the assets of a town like mine with 120,000 residents could have a value as high as $500 million. As well as managing the $500 million of energy assets there is likely to be debt funding of $300 million. This makes each town very attractive to win the management contract for.


But software is the key enabler. So a platform is needed that can:

  • Do billing to microgrid members
  • Manage communication
  • Manage demand and production
  • Manage maintenance and construction of energy assets

To make it more clear this is not a monopoly. Members of the town can be outside of the microgrid. The Microgrid represents the production and demand of its members only, effectively it can negotiate with the national grid on their behalf.

The microgrid will have smart meters in members homes and businesses monitoring their production and demand and making predictions of what they can sell to and from the national grid. The lowest cost energy will be energy the microgrid members make themselves so the microgrid will aim to be self sufficient but at time it will sell and at time buy energy.


Comparing conventional energy companies to Microgrids:

  • They don't have local democratic control of prices and are not trusted.
  • They do not have any incentives to encourage building local production.
  • They lack the local relationships to encourage active participation in demand management.
  • Local production requires people to commit their private space, it is better done locally.

Key concepts involved:

  • The first key concept is a common return on investment across energy assets so that the grid can be planned optimally at the local level. Space owners invest space and cash into a limited liability partnership that owns the energy assets - solar panels, batteries etc. The operations and management are done by a private outsourced company.
  • A second key concept is democratic control over pricing. Billing is made by town billing company, owned by the local government.
  • The third key concept is that this is not a monopoly, not everyone has to join in. The customers demand on the main grid is managed as a block but people in the town can be outside of this.
  • Software is SAAS: billing, grid management, demand management, energy asset management. This paid for by the limited liability partnership.

Why does this matter:

  • Low cost energy
  • Green energy
  • Reliable energy
